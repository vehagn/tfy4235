Linear Programming

Canonical form

$$ \begin{align} & \text{maximize} && Z = \sum_{i=1}^{N}c_{i} x_{i} \\ & \text{subject to} && A_{ij} ~x_j \leq b_i, i = 1,..,M_1 \\ & && A_{ij} ~x_j \geq b_i,i = M_1+1,..,N \\ & \text{and} && x_j \ge 0 \end{align} $$

where $N = M_1 + M_2$. The function $Z$ (called the objective function) is a linear function in $x_i$ on the area restricted by the set of linear inequalities, and for positive values of the variables. I have used the Einstein convention for summation over repeated incides.

For linear programming, the minimum (or maximum) of the function will always be on a vertex, i.e. a corner of the defined area (if a unique solution exists). This is because the level sets are hyperplanes for linear systems.

Simplex algorithm

I chose to do this a little different than the lecture notes. Hopefully it's good to look at it in different ways.

The first part of the Simplex algorithm is to modify the original linear program by introducing new variables to the inequalities such that each inequality turn it into an equality:

$$ \begin{align} & \text{maximize} && Z = \sum_{i=1}^{N}c_{i} x_{i} \\ & \text{subject to} && A_{ij} ~x_j + s_i = b_i,~ i = 1,\ldots,M_1 \\ & && A_{ij} ~x_j - s_i = b_i,~ i = M_1+1,\ldots N \\ & \text{and} && x_j \ge 0, s_i \ge 0 \end{align} $$

Example:

Consider the linear program

$$Z = -2 x - 3 y - 4 z$$

given the inequalities

$$\begin{align} 3 x + 2 y + z &\le 10\\ 2 x + 5 y + 3 z &\le 15\\ x,y,z &\ge 0 \end{align}$$

As an observation, notice that since all the coefficients in the objective function function is negative, we can find the maximum value of the function by the trivial solution $x_i = 0$.

Therefore, if we had all the coefficients of the objective function were positive, we could find the minimum by setting all variables to zero.

Modification

we modify this to

$$\begin{align} 3 x + 2 y + z + s_1& = 10\\ 2 x + 5 y + 3 z + s_2 & = 15\\ x,y,z &\ge 0 \end{align}$$

We represent this with an augmented matrix

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 10 \\ 0 & 2 & 5 & 3 & 0 & 1 & 15 \end{array}\right) $$

where the first row corresponds to

$$ 1 \cdot Z + 2 \cdot x + 3 \cdot y + 4 \cdot z + 0 \cdot s_1 + 0 \cdot s_2 = 0 $$

and similar for the other equations. We note that all the coefficients in the upper row (except for the column for $Z$), are positive.

To find the minimum, we need to turn all of the coefficients in the upper row negative. This is what the Simplex algorithm does, using (essentially) Gaussian elimination with pivoting.

A solution that satisfies all the inequalities is $x=y=z=0, s_1=10, s_2 = 15~$ (called a basic feasible solution). We choose any of the variables (x,y,z) and choose one of the non-objective equations. We use this equation to eliminate the chosen variable in the remaining equations.

In this case we choose $z$, and the 3rd row to pivot. First, normalize the third row to the $z$-column:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccccc|c}
1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 & 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 & 10 \\ 0 & \frac{2}{3} & \frac{5}{3} & 1 & 0 & \frac{1}{3} & 5 \end{array}\right) $$

subtracting one times the third row from the second row, and four times the third row from the first row, gives

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccccc|c}
1 & -\frac{2}{3} & -\frac{11}{3} & 0 & 0 & -\frac{4}{3} & -20 \\ 0 & \frac{7}{3} & \frac{1}{3} & 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{3} & 5 \\ 0 & \frac{2}{3} & \frac{5}{3} & 1 & 0 & \frac{1}{3} & 5 \end{array}\right) $$

Now, the first row corresponds to

$$ Z = -20 + \frac{2}{3} x + \frac{11}{3} y + \frac{4}{3} s_2 $$

which has the minimum value $Z = -20$Â for $x=y=s_2=0$ .

Since this form of the objective function is equivalent to the original form, we have found the solution of the problem. We were lucky in this case. In the general case, we may need to do several pivots. For each pivot we move from one BFS to a better BFS. Which variable and row we choose pivot will affect the speed of which we find the best solution.

Note that the BFS's corresponds to the corners of the region governed by the inequalities.
